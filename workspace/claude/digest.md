# Deep Digest: Blockchain

## Core architecture

A blockchain is an append-only, distributed ledger structured as a sequence of blocks. Each block contains a set of records (typically transactions), a timestamp, a nonce, and — crucially — the cryptographic hash of the preceding block. Because any change to a historical block alters its hash, which in turn invalidates every subsequent block's reference, the chain is structurally resistant to silent modification. Within each block, records are organized into a Merkle tree: a binary tree of hashes that allows any single record to be verified against the block header without downloading the full dataset. This combination of chained hashes and Merkle proofs enables lightweight clients to verify inclusion proofs without trusting a central server, a property called simplified payment verification (SPV).

## Consensus mechanisms

Agreement on the canonical chain in a network of mutually distrusting nodes is the hardest problem blockchain solves. Proof-of-work (PoW), pioneered by Bitcoin, requires nodes to expend real computational energy to propose blocks, making rewriting history expensive proportional to honest network hash rate. The downside is energy consumption at scale. Proof-of-stake (PoS), adopted by Ethereum in 2022, replaces energy with economic collateral: validators lock up capital that can be slashed if they act dishonestly. PoS achieves comparable security at a fraction of the energy cost but introduces different attack vectors around capital concentration. Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT) variants — used in permissioned networks like Hyperledger Fabric and Cosmos chains — achieve fast finality in smaller, known validator sets at the cost of decentralization. The right consensus mechanism depends entirely on the threat model and performance requirements of the specific application.

## Decentralization and the trust model

The foundational value proposition of public blockchains is the replacement of institutional trust with verifiable cryptographic guarantees. Any participant can run a full node, independently verify every transaction since genesis, and enforce the protocol rules without permission from any authority. This property eliminates single points of failure and censorship, but governance — deciding how the protocol itself evolves — becomes a coordination problem. Protocol upgrades happen through rough social consensus among developers, miners or validators, and economic nodes. When consensus breaks down irrecoverably, chains fork: Bitcoin Cash split from Bitcoin in 2017 over block size; Ethereum Classic split from Ethereum in 2016 over a disputed fund recovery. Forks are both a safety valve and a risk: they preserve minority rights but dilute network effects.

## Smart contracts and programmability

Ethereum introduced the concept of a Turing-complete virtual machine running on top of a blockchain. Smart contracts are programs stored immutably on-chain whose execution is deterministic, permissionless, and guaranteed by the full validator set. The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) has become the dominant runtime, with compatible implementations deployed on Polygon, Avalanche, BNB Chain, and dozens of other networks. Gas — a unit denominating computation cost — prevents infinite loops and aligns economic incentives: users pay for what they consume. Smart contracts enable decentralized finance protocols (lending, trading, derivatives without brokers), non-fungible tokens, decentralized autonomous organizations, and any application that benefits from trustless settlement. The composability of contracts — one protocol calling another atomically in a single transaction — creates emergent financial systems that no central planner designed.

## Scalability challenges

Public blockchains face a trilemma: maximizing security, decentralization, and throughput simultaneously appears to require sacrificing one. Bitcoin processes roughly 7 transactions per second; Ethereum's base layer around 15. Visa handles tens of thousands. Layer-2 solutions move execution off-chain while anchoring security to the base layer. Optimistic rollups (Arbitrum, Optimism) batch transactions and post compressed proofs to L1, assuming correctness unless challenged within a dispute window. ZK-rollups (StarkNet, zkSync) generate cryptographic validity proofs that allow L1 to verify correctness without re-executing transactions. Sharding — partitioning the validator set so each shard processes a subset of transactions in parallel — is an architectural approach to scaling L1 itself. These solutions collectively push practical throughput into the thousands of TPS range, but user experience complexity and liquidity fragmentation remain real costs.

## Real-world adoption and limitations

Enterprise adoption has generally favored permissioned variants — Hyperledger Fabric, R3 Corda — where participants are known, throughput requirements are higher, and privacy between competitors is non-negotiable. These networks sacrifice the censorship-resistance of public chains in exchange for performance and compliance. Public blockchain adoption has grown most visibly in financial services: spot Bitcoin ETFs approved in the US in 2024 brought institutional capital directly into the asset class; tokenized government bonds and money market funds on Ethereum are now managed by institutions including BlackRock. Persistent limitations include user-key management complexity (loss of private keys means permanent loss of assets), smart contract vulnerability (billions lost to code exploits since 2016), regulatory uncertainty across jurisdictions, and the fundamental tension between transparency and financial privacy.
