> Topic: blockchain

Blockchain is a distributed ledger technology that stores data in linked, cryptographically secured blocks. Each block contains a hash of the previous block, making the chain tamper-evident: altering one block invalidates every block after it. This structure removes the need for a central authority, as any participant in the network can independently verify the full history of records, whether those records are financial transactions, contracts, or other data.

Bitcoin, launched in 2009, was the first large-scale application of a blockchain and introduced proof-of-work consensus — a mechanism where nodes compete to solve computationally expensive puzzles to earn the right to append the next block. This design makes rewriting history prohibitively expensive, since an attacker would need to outpace the honest majority of the network's combined computing power, a threshold known as the 51% attack barrier.

Ethereum extended the model by adding smart contracts: self-executing programs stored on-chain that run exactly as coded without any possibility of downtime, censorship, or third-party interference. Smart contracts underpin decentralized finance (DeFi), non-fungible tokens (NFTs), and decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs), turning a blockchain from a simple ledger into a programmable global settlement layer capable of encoding arbitrary business logic.
