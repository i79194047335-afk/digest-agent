## Core Protocols & Mechanics

Decentralized finance rebuilds traditional financial services as open, permissionless software running on public blockchains. At its foundation are smart contracts — autonomous code that executes financial logic without requiring banks or brokers. Trades happen inside liquidity pools governed by Automated Market Makers: instead of matching buyers and sellers through order books, AMMs hold reserves of two tokens and price them using algorithmic formulas. Uniswap popularized the constant-product model, where the product of both reserves stays fixed, ensuring any trade moves price according to depth. Lending markets like Aave and Compound extend this permissionless model to credit: users deposit collateral and borrow against it, with interest rates adjusted continuously by on-chain supply-and-demand curves. Yield farming layers atop these primitives, allowing users to deposit capital across multiple protocols simultaneously, stacking rewards from fees and token incentives. Together these building blocks replicate the full range of banking functions — trading, lending, earning — entirely through code.

## Economic Model & Tokenomics

The economic architecture of DeFi centers on aligning the interests of users, liquidity providers, and protocol developers through token-based incentives. Governance tokens give holders the right to propose and vote on protocol upgrades, fee changes, and treasury spending, distributing control away from any central team. Revenue flows in as protocol fees — typically a fraction of each swap or the interest spread on loans — and is split between liquidity providers who take the majority and token holders who receive buybacks or dividends. Total Value Locked measures the capital committed to a protocol at any moment; high TVL signals user confidence and generates more fee income. To bootstrap liquidity before organic demand takes hold, protocols run liquidity mining campaigns that pay depositors in native tokens. These campaigns generate rapid growth but carry an inflationary cost: early reward tokens are often sold immediately, creating sell pressure that erodes token value. Sustainable DeFi economics therefore require transitioning from emissions-driven growth toward genuine fee revenue that justifies token valuations over the long term.

## Risks & Regulatory Landscape

DeFi's open and composable architecture creates a unique risk surface that distinguishes it from traditional finance. Smart contract vulnerabilities remain the most direct threat: a single logic error can be exploited to drain an entire protocol, and billions of dollars have been lost to flash loan attacks, reentrancy bugs, and price oracle manipulation. Rug pulls represent a social risk: anonymous developers attract deposits into new protocols and then disappear with user funds, a scam made easy by low deployment costs and pseudonymity. Composability amplifies both benefits and dangers — the same interconnection that lets users chain protocols for higher yield also means one protocol's failure can cascade into another's insolvency. On the regulatory front, authorities globally are moving to assert jurisdiction. The U.S. SEC and CFTC have brought enforcement actions treating DeFi token issuances as unregistered securities offerings, while the European Union's Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation established the first comprehensive framework, though it largely exempts protocols that are genuinely decentralized and lack an identifiable issuer.
