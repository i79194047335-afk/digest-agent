## Core Protocols

Decentralized finance is built upon a set of open, self-executing protocols that together replicate the functions of traditional financial services without any central intermediary. Automated market makers sit at the foundation, replacing conventional order books with algorithmic liquidity pools where token prices emerge from mathematical formulas rather than matching engines. Any participant can supply capital by depositing token pairs into a pool and earning a proportional share of the trading fees generated. Permissionless lending platforms extend the model to credit: borrowers lock collateral on-chain and receive funds instantly, with interest rates adjusting in real time to pool utilization. Stablecoins serve as the ecosystem's unit of account and come in three forms — fiat-backed, crypto-backed, and algorithmic — each with its own trust and capital-efficiency trade-offs. Composability binds these layers: protocols share open interfaces, allowing developers to combine multiple services in one atomic transaction and assemble highly complex instruments from simple, auditable pieces.

## Governance & Tokenomics

Governing a decentralized protocol without a corporate hierarchy requires a new set of coordination tools. Most large DeFi systems are controlled by decentralized autonomous organizations — DAOs — which make decisions through on-chain proposal-and-vote mechanisms. Any holder of a governance token can submit or vote on proposals that adjust protocol parameters, upgrade smart contracts, or reallocate treasury funds. Token weighting creates visible inequalities: large holders wield outsized influence, and low participation often concentrates power among a small active group. Delegation mechanisms partially address this by allowing passive holders to transfer their voting power to informed delegates without surrendering token ownership. On the incentive side, liquidity mining programs emit newly minted governance tokens to users who supply capital, rapidly bootstrapping total value locked but often attracting mercenary participants who exit once rewards diminish. Long-term protocol health therefore depends heavily on emission schedules and whether tokens accrue genuine value through fee-sharing or token buybacks.

## Risks & Challenges

Decentralized finance offers open access and radical transparency, but it also concentrates several severe risks that traditional systems have either solved or intermediated away. Smart contract vulnerabilities remain the dominant threat: even professionally audited code has lost hundreds of millions of dollars to exploits targeting subtle edge-case logic bugs. Oracle manipulation compounds the danger — false price data fed to a lending protocol can drain collateral pools instantly. Liquidity providers face a subtler hazard called impermanent loss: when the ratio of their deposited tokens shifts materially against them, they would have earned more by simply holding the assets outright. Regulatory uncertainty hangs over the entire sector, as jurisdictions disagree on whether governance tokens are securities and how to reach offshore protocol teams. Finally, composability — DeFi's greatest structural strength — doubles as a systemic risk: one protocol failure can cascade through interconnected positions and destabilize the broader ecosystem within a single Ethereum block.
