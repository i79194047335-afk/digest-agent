> Topic: Arbitrage on DEXs

## AMM Mechanics & Price Formation

Constant-function market makers like Uniswap enforce a constant product invariant determining the price of one asset relative to another within a single pool. This deterministic price frequently diverges from the market rate on centralized exchanges or deeper liquidity venues, creating the raw material for arbitrage. Concentrated liquidity models, which pool capital within a narrow price band, amplify this effect: when the market price exits that band, divergence becomes steep and immediate. The non-linear slippage curve inherent to CFMMs means larger trades incur proportionally greater price impact — precisely the gradient arbitrageurs ride to restore equilibrium. By trading against the pool until its internal price aligns with the external reference, arbitrageurs effectively rebalance the AMM, a feature of the design and a cost to passive liquidity providers. Deeper pools dampen divergence but demand larger capital outlays to move the price meaningfully and reshape the opportunity set continuously.

## Arbitrage Strategies & Execution Infrastructure

Arbitrageurs operate across three distinct structural dimensions. DEX-to-DEX arbitrage targets price differences for identical assets listed on different decentralized exchanges, exploiting the lag in how quickly each pool's price updates. CEX-to-DEX arbitrage bridges centralized and decentralized markets, capturing the spread between exchange rates on venues with fundamentally different latency and liquidity profiles. Cross-chain arbitrage extends the same logic across distinct blockchain networks, relying on bridges or atomic swaps to move value between domains within a single transaction window. Flash loans supercharge these strategies by providing uncollateralized capital that is borrowed and repaid atomically within one block, removing the need for the arbitrageur to commit their own funds. On the execution side, priority gas auctions allow searchers to bid for transaction ordering, effectively purchasing the right to settle before competitors. Together these pieces form an infrastructure stack that determines who captures an opportunity and at what speed.

## Risk, Competition & Profitability Economics

The economics of DEX arbitrage have shifted dramatically as the ecosystem matured. MEV searchers compete aggressively by bidding up priority gas fees, a dynamic that asymptotically drives net arbitrage profits toward zero for each opportunity. Sandwich attacks compound the pressure: a malicious searcher front-runs the arbitrage transaction, pushes the price further, then back-runs it to extract value that would have belonged to the original arbitrageur. Failed transactions caused by slippage breaches, stale quotes, or front-running still consume gas fees, making revert risk a direct profitability drain. Cross-domain MEV has intensified competition as solvers now compete across Layer 2 networks and the base layer simultaneously, fragmenting opportunity sets while unifying the searcher pool. By 2026 the cumulative effect is clear: algorithmic efficiency and searcher saturation have compressed margins to thin levels, making execution speed, latency optimization, and capital efficiency the primary differentiators between profitable and unprofitable searchers.
