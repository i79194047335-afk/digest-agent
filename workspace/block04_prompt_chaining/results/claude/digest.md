# Дайджест: трейдинг и финансовые рынки — от форекс до криптовалюты

## Forex market: structure, major currency pairs, and key drivers of price movement

The foreign exchange market is the largest and most liquid financial market in the world, with a daily turnover exceeding $7.5 trillion. Unlike stock exchanges, forex operates over the counter, running continuously across global financial centers — Tokyo, London, New York — five days a week. The market is structured in tiers: central banks and commercial banks at the top, followed by institutional investors, hedge funds, and retail brokers. Major pairs such as EUR/USD, GBP/USD, and USD/JPY account for the bulk of volume and offer the tightest spreads. Price movement is driven by macroeconomic data releases — inflation figures, employment reports, GDP growth — as well as central bank interest rate decisions and geopolitical developments. Currency correlations also play a role: commodity-linked currencies like AUD and CAD track energy and metals prices closely. Understanding intermarket relationships is essential. Retail traders typically access the market through leveraged contracts for difference or spot accounts, making risk management a primary concern given the speed and scale of intraday moves.

## Stock and derivatives markets: equities, futures, and options in global trading

Equity markets allow investors to purchase ownership stakes in publicly listed companies, with major venues including the NYSE, NASDAQ, London Stock Exchange, and Tokyo Stock Exchange. Prices reflect earnings expectations, macroeconomic sentiment, and sector rotations driven by interest rate cycles. During risk-on periods, growth stocks outperform; during tightening cycles, value and defensive sectors tend to lead. Derivatives markets layer additional complexity and opportunity onto this foundation. Futures contracts allow participants to speculate on or hedge exposure to indices, commodities, currencies, and interest rates with standardized terms and centralized clearing. Options provide asymmetric payoff structures — buyers acquire rights without obligations, enabling defined-risk strategies like protective puts or covered calls. Institutional participants use derivatives extensively for portfolio hedging, while retail traders increasingly access these instruments through zero-commission platforms. Market structure has shifted significantly with the rise of passive index investing and algorithmic execution, compressing spreads and increasing intraday correlation across asset classes. Understanding sector dynamics, earnings cycles, and volatility regimes is foundational to effective equity and derivatives trading.

## Cryptocurrency markets: Bitcoin, altcoins, liquidity, and market cycles

Cryptocurrency markets have matured considerably since Bitcoin's 2009 launch but remain structurally distinct from traditional finance. Bitcoin functions as the market's reserve asset — its four-year halving cycle, which reduces miner block rewards by 50%, has historically correlated with major bull and bear phases. Altcoins, including Ethereum and a broad ecosystem of layer-1 and layer-2 protocols, tend to amplify Bitcoin's directional moves with higher volatility. Liquidity is fragmented across dozens of centralized exchanges and a growing number of decentralized protocols, creating arbitrage opportunities but also increasing slippage risk during volatile episodes. Market cycles in crypto are driven by a combination of on-chain metrics — such as realized profit and loss, exchange inflows, and miner capitulation signals — and broader macro factors like dollar strength and Federal Reserve policy. The collapse of major players including FTX in 2022 demonstrated the systemic risk embedded in under-collateralized exchange models, accelerating demand for regulated custodians and transparent proof-of-reserves. Institutional adoption through spot Bitcoin ETFs, approved in the United States in early 2024, has introduced new demand dynamics and reduced the dominance of purely speculative retail flows.

## Trading strategies and risk management: technical analysis, algorithmic trading, and position sizing

Successful trading integrates strategy selection, systematic execution, and disciplined risk control. Technical analysis remains widely used across asset classes, with practitioners relying on price action, trend-following indicators such as moving averages and MACD, and mean-reversion tools including RSI and Bollinger Bands. Support and resistance levels, order flow analysis, and volume profile provide structural context for entries and exits. Algorithmic trading has become dominant in institutional environments, where latency advantages and systematic rule-following reduce emotional decision-making. Retail algorithmic traders increasingly deploy bots on platforms such as MetaTrader or through exchange APIs, running strategies from simple moving average crossovers to complex machine learning models. Risk management is the non-negotiable foundation of longevity in markets. Position sizing methods — fixed fractional, Kelly criterion, volatility-adjusted — determine how much capital is exposed on any single trade. Stop-loss discipline prevents small losing trades from becoming catastrophic drawdowns. Diversification across uncorrelated instruments, combined with a clearly defined maximum daily loss threshold, allows traders to survive adverse conditions and remain active long enough to capture favorable periods. Consistent process outperforms inconsistent brilliance over time.

## Regulation and the future of financial markets: DeFi, tokenization, and evolving oversight

Financial market regulation is undergoing a period of significant evolution driven by the emergence of decentralized finance and the tokenization of real-world assets. Traditional markets operate under well-established frameworks — the SEC and CFTC in the United States, ESMA in Europe, FCA in the United Kingdom — that enforce disclosure requirements, market integrity standards, and investor protections. Cryptocurrency initially operated in a regulatory gray zone, but enforcement actions, exchange failures, and the mainstreaming of digital assets have forced regulators to act. The EU's MiCA framework, fully effective from 2025, sets a comprehensive standard for crypto-asset issuers and service providers. Decentralized finance protocols, which facilitate lending, trading, and yield generation without intermediaries, present a distinct challenge: no legal entity to hold accountable and no central point of control. Regulators are exploring on-chain identity, smart contract audits, and protocol-level compliance mechanisms. Meanwhile, tokenization of traditional assets — equities, bonds, real estate, commodities — promises to increase market access, reduce settlement times, and enable fractional ownership. Major financial institutions including BlackRock and JPMorgan are actively building tokenized product infrastructure, signaling that the convergence of traditional and decentralized finance is a structural shift rather than a speculative trend.
